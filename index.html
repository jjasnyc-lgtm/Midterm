01 — About the Project

What This Project Is About

This project presents a redesign proposal for Montefiore Square / Montefiore Park, located in East Harlem, New York City. The plaza sits at a critical intersection in one of the most culturally rich neighborhoods in the city — yet its current state fails to serve the community it belongs to.

Using AI-assisted image generation, site research, NYC DOT plaza references, and design thinking, this project proposes a transformative redesign centered on green wellness, accessibility, community programming, and pedestrian safety.

Project Track Goals
🎯

Essential Question

How might we redesign Montefiore Square to become a safe, green, and inclusive community hub for East Harlem residents?

👥

Primary Users

Seniors, families with children, commuters, youth, and local residents who currently avoid or underuse the space.

📍

Location

Montefiore Square, East Harlem, NYC — at the intersection of E 138th St and Willis Ave, near the 138th St/Grand Concourse subway station.

🛠️

Deliverable

A complete redesign concept including site analysis, AI-generated renderings, and a final proposal with justification.

02 — The Problem

What's Not Working

Montefiore Square is a missed opportunity. Despite sitting in a densely populated neighborhood with high foot traffic, the plaza functions as little more than a concrete pass-through — uninviting, underused, and disconnected from the community.

Core Issues Identified

Through site observation, Google Maps street view analysis, and NYC DOT project research, the following critical problems were identified:

🚗 Pedestrian-Vehicle Conflict

The plaza edges are poorly defined, creating ambiguous zones where pedestrians and vehicles compete for space. Crossing points lack adequate signaling and protection.

Safety Risk Poor Signage

🌫️ Lack of Green Space

The hardscape-dominated plaza offers minimal shade, no meaningful planting, and no ecological value. In summer heat, the plaza becomes actively uncomfortable.

Heat Island No Canopy

♿ Accessibility Gaps

Uneven surfaces, lack of tactile paving, and missing resting points make the space difficult to navigate for seniors and people with disabilities.

ADA Issues No Rest Nodes

📵 No Programming or Activation

The plaza lacks any infrastructure for events, markets, or community gathering. There are no flexible surfaces, kiosks, or programming zones — leaving it empty most of the day.

Underactivated No Amenities
03 — Site & Context

Understanding the Place

Montefiore Square is embedded in a neighborhood with rich cultural heritage and significant community need. Understanding who uses this space — and who should — is central to the redesign.

Site Observations

🚶 Movement Patterns

High pedestrian flow during morning and evening commute hours. People use the plaza primarily as a cut-through, not a destination. No clear desire lines are accommodated by current paving.

🏘️ Community Profile

The surrounding blocks include multi-family residential buildings, a high school, senior housing, and local small businesses — a mix that calls for multi-generational programming.

🌤️ Microclimate

Sun exposure is high due to surrounding low-rise context. No trees or overhead structure exist to create shade. Wind channeling along the avenue makes the space uncomfortable in cooler months.

🔊 Sound & Safety

Traffic noise from the adjacent avenue is significant. The plaza lacks spatial separation from traffic. Evening lighting is insufficient, contributing to perceived and actual safety concerns after dark.

Design References

This proposal was informed by NYC DOT plaza projects including Westchester Sq Plaza, Myrtle/Wyckoff Plaza, and Hillel Place — all of which demonstrate how underutilized hardscape can become active community anchors through strategic planting, seating, and programming infrastructure.

04 — Design Options

Three Directions Explored

Before arriving at the final proposal, three distinct design directions were developed and evaluated. Each was generated with a different conceptual intent and visual identity.

🌿

Option A — Green Wellness Plaza

A nature-forward redesign with shade trees, seasonal planting beds, flexible lawn areas, and a central wellness garden. Focused on rest, calm, and ecological value.

Shade Canopy Native Plants Lawn Zones
🏪

Option B — Community Market Plaza

An activated market concept with vendor kiosk zones, public art installations, weekend programming infrastructure, and seating oriented around social commerce.

Vendor Zones Public Art Event Ready
🚶

Option C — Safe Pedestrian Hub ✓

Selected. A pedestrian-first redesign that combines improved crossings, green buffer planting, accessible seating nodes, lighting infrastructure, and flexible community space. The strongest balance of safety, green, and activation.

05 — Final Proposal

The Redesign: A Pedestrian-Green Community Hub

The selected proposal transforms Montefiore Square into a safe, green, and socially active plaza for East Harlem residents. It draws on the strongest elements of all three design options and is grounded in the site's specific movement patterns, user needs, and cultural context.

🌳

AI-generated redesign rendering — pedestrian-centered green plaza with shade canopy and community seating

Key Design Moves

  • Shade Tree Canopy: 8–10 mature street trees planted along the plaza perimeter and interior, reducing heat island effect and creating comfortable year-round zones.
  • Protected Pedestrian Crossings: Raised crosswalks with clear paint, planted medians, and improved signal timing at all four entry points.
  • Accessible Seating Nodes: Three seating alcoves with mixed bench types (backed, backless, armrest-equipped) positioned at shaded rest points along main desire lines.
  • Flexible Gathering Surface: A central level paved area with power access for weekend markets, cultural events, and community programming.
  • Cultural Planting Palette: Native and culturally resonant plantings — including bougainvillea trellis reference, seasonal color, and Harlem-inspired mosaic tile edging.
  • Lighting Infrastructure: Pedestrian-scale bollard lights and overhead string lighting for evening activation and safety.
Design Justification

This proposal was selected because it is the most holistic response to the plaza's real problems. It addresses safety without removing activity, adds green without sacrificing flexibility, and creates a sense of identity without imposing a single aesthetic. It is implementable within existing DOT plaza improvement frameworks and can be phased over time.

06 — Reflection & Conclusion

Human Judgment + AI Assistance

This project used AI image generation (DALL·E) as a design exploration tool — not a replacement for design thinking. Understanding where AI helped and where human judgment was essential is critical to evaluating the work honestly.

What AI Helped With

AI image generation was highly effective at rapidly visualizing multiple design directions — producing before/after renderings, aerial concept views, and material explorations in minutes rather than hours.

It allowed for iterative comparison across fundamentally different design concepts without committing to any single direction prematurely.

AI also helped articulate spatial atmosphere — the "feel" of a place at different times of day and seasons — which is difficult to sketch quickly.

What Required Human Judgment

Prompting effectively required deep knowledge of the site. Generic prompts produced generic results. Useful images only emerged after studying the actual plaza through Google Maps, NYC DOT references, and site analysis.

Selecting between options required design judgment — understanding what a community actually needs, not what looks most dramatic in a rendering.

Writing the justification, interpreting the analysis, and connecting the design to real user needs was entirely human work that AI could not replicate.

Final Reflection

Montefiore Square doesn't need to be extraordinary to be meaningful. It needs to be safe, shaded, and welcoming for the people who walk through it every day. This project showed that even a small plaza intervention — done thoughtfully and grounded in real community needs — can significantly improve the quality of daily life in East Harlem. The AI tools accelerated the visual exploration, but the design thinking, site understanding, and community-centered decisions were made by the designer.
